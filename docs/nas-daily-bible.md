# Nas Daily Bible

## Purpose

This file defines how the dashboard should decide:

1. Which stories are worth showing at all
2. How each story should be written so the team feels excited to make it

The goal is not to imitate Nas Daily word-for-word. The goal is to capture the editorial logic:
- highly human
- easy to understand fast
- emotionally sharp
- surprising
- visual
- globally relatable
- specific enough to make content immediately

## Core Belief

People do not share information just because it is useful.
They share stories that make them feel:
- surprise
- admiration
- disbelief
- urgency
- hope
- tension
- inspiration
- curiosity

Every story should make a team member think:
"I can already imagine the first frame of the video."

## Story Selection Rules

Only show stories that pass most of these tests:

### 1. Human First

Prefer stories built around:
- one person
- one family
- one team
- one conflict
- one dramatic decision

Avoid stories that are only:
- abstract trends
- generic announcements
- corporate talking points
- vague market commentary

### 2. Big Tension In A Small Frame

Choose stories where a large idea can be understood through a tiny moment.

Good examples:
- one student choosing a cheaper country over a dream destination
- one medic using fast communication to save someone
- one founder turning waste into food or software into access

Weak examples:
- “industry leaders gathered at a summit”
- “a conference discussed innovation”
- “a brand announced its strategy”

### 3. Surprise Or Contradiction

Prefer stories with:
- irony
- contrast
- contradiction
- a counterintuitive twist
- extreme stakes

Examples of strong contrast:
- tiny update, huge consequence
- boring policy, massive impact
- ordinary person, extraordinary result
- local story, global meaning

Even better:
- a school that does not teach
- garbage that does not smell
- a tiny country doing something huge
- a normal person doing something impossible
- a rule that creates the opposite result

If a story contains a contradiction people can understand in one second, it becomes much stronger.

### 3B. Superlatives Win Attention

Strong Nas Daily-style stories often contain:
- the tallest
- the shortest
- the biggest
- the smallest
- the highest
- the lowest
- the most
- the least
- the fastest
- the slowest
- the first
- the last
- the only

Prefer stories that contain:
- an extreme number
- an extreme size
- an extreme comparison
- a record
- a first-ever
- a last-chance moment

If two stories are equally relevant, prefer the one with the sharper extreme.

### 4. Visual Potential

A story is stronger if the team can imagine:
- the face
- the setting
- the turning point
- the before/after
- the object, scene, or number that anchors the story

Avoid stories that are hard to picture.

### 5. Broad Human Relevance

Even when a story is client-specific, it should still touch something broadly understandable:
- ambition
- fear
- money
- status
- family
- health
- survival
- belonging
- identity
- hope

### 6. Freshness

For time-sensitive content:
- prefer today first
- then the last 7 days
- never include older items unless explicitly framed as evergreen context elsewhere

### 7. News Worth Piggybacking On

For time-sensitive content, prefer:
- policy changes
- regulation
- medical or science breakthroughs
- government decisions
- consumer behavior shifts
- global cultural moments
- sports moments
- entertainment moments
- startup/funding/competition moves
- safety, crisis, or urgency stories
- market signals that affect real people

Avoid over-indexing on:
- conferences
- panels
- keynote recaps
- trade shows
- “summit” content with no concrete consequence

Only use an event if it created:
- a real deadline
- a real market signal
- a real policy shift
- a real human consequence
- or a clear reason an audience would care right now

## Story Rejection Rules

Reject stories if they are:
- too abstract
- too promotional
- too old
- too niche without human stakes
- too repetitive with other stories already shown
- weakly related to the client
- hard to visualize
- based only on event marketing pages or fluff articles

If a story sounds informative but not exciting, reject it.

## Writing Rules

### 1. Titles Must Earn The Click

The title should feel like a content title, not a headline copied from the news.

Good title qualities:
- short
- sharp
- surprising
- high-contrast
- emotionally loaded
- easy to say out loud
- understandable by a 13-year-old

The best titles usually do one of these four things:
- use a superlative
- present a contradiction
- answer a question
- compress a huge story into one simple line

### 1A. Question Titles Are Powerful

Nas Daily-style titles often work well when they answer a simple question people already want to ask.

Strong opening words:
- How
- Why
- What

Good examples:
- How did this student get in with no money?
- Why is this tiny country beating everyone?
- What makes this garbage not smell?
- Why are students avoiding the U.S. now?

Question titles work best when:
- the question is simple
- the answer is surprising
- the answer matters to many people

### 1B. Contradiction Titles Are Powerful

Very strong titles often sound almost impossible at first.

Examples:
- The school that does not teach
- The garbage that does not smell
- The country getting richer by using less
- The dentist story that is really about sleep

If a contradiction is real and easy to understand, use it.

### 1C. Superlative Titles Are Powerful

Strong examples:
- The biggest change nobody noticed
- The smallest deadline with the biggest consequences
- The highest-paying job this student almost missed
- The most expensive mistake in crypto

When possible, replace weak words with stronger extremes.

Weak:
- important
- interesting
- useful

Stronger:
- biggest
- smallest
- fastest
- rarest
- first
- last
- only
- most
- least

Preferred patterns:
- This student did X
- This guy turned X into Y
- How did X happen?
- Why is X changing now?
- What makes this so strange?
- The boring update that could affect millions
- The good news that comes with a warning
- The smallest deadline with the biggest consequences
- The quiet crisis everyone will notice too late
- The weird story hiding inside a normal headline

Avoid:
- article-like titles
- technical titles
- corporate phrasing
- titles with jargon overload
- repeated formulas across many cards

### 2. Summary Writing

The paragraph should:
- explain what happened clearly
- tell the human angle fast
- include the specific twist or stake
- stay factual and sourced
- make the team want to create the content immediately

The summary should read like:
- a concise content brief
- not a Wikipedia entry
- not a newsroom transcript

### 2A. Keep It At 8th Grade Level

Use language that is:
- simple
- clear
- spoken
- direct

Assume the audience should understand the story in one fast read.

Prefer:
- simple words over technical words
- short sentences over long sentences
- one clear idea per sentence
- active voice over passive voice

Avoid:
- jargon
- consultant language
- academic phrasing
- long dependent clauses
- complicated explanations that kill momentum

If a 13-year-old cannot understand it quickly, rewrite it.

### 3. Present The Hook, Not Just The Facts

When presenting a story, emphasize:
- what is unexpected
- what makes people care
- what makes this visual
- what makes this timely

### 4. Why This Could Go Viral

Do not say vague things like:
- “it is interesting”
- “it is engaging”
- “it could resonate”

Instead explain:
- what specific emotion drives sharing
- what the social hook is
- what part of the story makes people retell it
- why someone would send it to a friend

### 5. Lesson

The lesson should feel:
- human
- clean
- memorable
- slightly bigger than the event itself

It should sound like something a creator would say at the end of a strong short video.

## Voice Rules

Use a tone that feels:
- energetic
- clear
- cinematic
- simple
- smart without sounding academic
- emotionally open
- exciting without sounding fake

The writing should make people feel:
- invited in
- curious
- united around a shared human reaction
- excited to tell the story to someone else

Whenever possible, frame stories in a way that brings people together around:
- admiration
- hope
- shared surprise
- shared humanity
- common curiosity

Do not write in a divisive, cynical, or cold way unless the story absolutely demands it.

Do not sound:
- robotic
- over-formal
- PR-like
- generic

## Final Test

Before returning a story, ask:

- Would a human stop scrolling for this?
- Can I picture the first shot?
- Is there a person, conflict, or twist?
- Is this actually fresh?
- Is the title strong enough to sound like a video, not an article?
- Does the wording feel simple enough for an 8th grader?
- Does it contain a superlative, contradiction, or sharp question when possible?
- Does it make people feel more connected, curious, or excited?
- Is this strong enough to make a creator say “we should post this today”?

If the answer is no, do not show the story.
